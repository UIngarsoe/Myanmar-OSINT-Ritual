🌿 Opening Reflection

Today is not merely the renewal of computer code.

Today is the renewal of Dāna (दान / ဒါန).

More than 2,500 years ago, the Buddha freely gave the Dhamma without asking ownership from those willing to learn. Likewise, this open-source donation represents the sharing of ideas so others may study, examine, improve, or respectfully disagree. In that spirit, knowledge is treated as something to be shared rather than possessed.

📖 Master Teaching One

Paṭiccasamuppāda (ပဋိစ္စသမုပ္ပာဒ)

📜 Pāli

"Yo paṭiccasamuppādaṃ passati, so dhammaṃ passati. Yo dhammaṃ passati, so paṭiccasamuppādaṃ passati."


---

🇬🇧 English

"He who sees Dependent Origination sees the Dhamma. He who sees the Dhamma sees Dependent Origination."

The Buddha did not teach that events happen randomly or through fate alone. Rather, phenomena arise because of conditions and cease when those conditions cease. This principle can also encourage designers of AI systems to examine causes, assumptions, and potential biases rather than relying only on outputs.

📖 Master Teaching Two

Avijjā (အဝိဇ္ဇာ)

📜 Pāli

"Avijjāpaccayā saṅkhārā."


---

🇬🇧 English

"Ignorance conditions formations."

In early Buddhism, Avijjā is not simply the absence of information. It is not seeing reality clearly. In technology, this can inspire developers to question incomplete data, hidden assumptions, or historical biases rather than accepting them uncritically. It is an ethical reminder, not a claim that AI literally possesses ignorance in the human sense.

📖 Master Teaching Three

Saṅkhārā (သင်္ခါရ)

📜 Pāli

"Cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi."


---

🇬🇧 English

"Monks, it is intention that I call kamma."

Actions are shaped by intention. Likewise, systems designed by humans reflect the intentions, assumptions, and priorities of their creators. Ethical design therefore begins before deployment—with careful reflection on purpose and consequences.

📖 Master Teaching Four

Yonisomanasikāra (ယောနိသောမနသိကာရ)

📜 Pāli

"Yoniso manasikāro kusalamūlaṃ."


---

🇬🇧 English

Wise attention investigates causes rather than reacting to appearances.

Before making a judgment, pause.

Before speaking, investigate.

Before sharing information, verify.

This habit protects both individuals and communities from error.

📖 Master Teaching Five

Poormanmeism

📜 Pāli

"Sabbe dhammā anattā."


---

🇬🇧 English

"All phenomena are not-self."

The phrase "I know nothing, I own nothing" expresses an attitude of intellectual humility. While it is not a canonical Buddhist quotation, it echoes the Buddhist encouragement to avoid attachment to fixed views and to remain open to learning.

📖 Master Teaching Six

Paññā (ပညာ)

📜 Pāli

"Paññāya passati."


---

🇬🇧 English

Wisdom is not measured by how much information one stores.

Wisdom is shown by seeing clearly, judging carefully, and acting compassionately.

Knowledge may build machines.

Wisdom guides how those machines are used.

📖 Master Teaching Seven

Mettā (မေတ္တာ)

📜 Pāli

"Sabbe sattā bhavantu sukhitattā."


---

🇬🇧 English

"May all beings be happy."

Technology should ultimately serve human well-being.

Compassion does not replace careful engineering, but it can inform the values and goals that guide responsible innovation.

🪷 Final Wisdom

📜 Pāli

"Appamādo amatapadaṃ."


---

🇬🇧 English

"Heedfulness is the path to the Deathless."

The deepest lesson of this lecture is not about replacing Buddhist teachings with algorithms. Instead, it is that the habits encouraged by the Buddha—careful attention, ethical reflection, humility, and compassion—can inspire how people think about technology and its impact on society. Technology changes quickly, but wisdom remains valuable across generations.
